About Municipality of Clarin

Clarin is a coastal municipality in the province of Misamis Occidental. The municipality has a land area of 75.73 square kilometers or 29.24 square miles which constitutes 3.77% of Misamis Occidental's total area. Its population as determined by the 2020 Census was 39,356. This represented 6.38% of the total population of Misamis Occidental province, or 0.78% of the overall population of the Northern Mindanao region. Based on these figures, the population density is computed at 520 inhabitants per square kilometer or 1,346 inhabitants per square mile.

Located in the northwest region of Panay Island, Kalibo occupies a strategic position as the primary transportation hub for the renowned resort island of Boracay. Blessed with a diverse array of attractions, the municipality beckons travelers with its captivating landscapes, cultural festivities, and eco-friendly initiatives.

At the heart of Kalibo's cultural heritage lies the iconic Ati-Atihan festival, a vibrant celebration of faith and camaraderie that draws visitors from far and wide. Additionally, Kalibo boasts the acclaimed Bakhawan Eco-Park, a semi-urban mangrove forest recognized for its environmental sustainability efforts and multiple accolades. Notably, Kalibo's tradition of piña-weaving, honored with a coveted inscription on the UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage List in 2023, reflects the town's enduring commitment to preserving its indigenous craftsmanship and artistic heritage.

The etymology of Kalibo traces back to its indigenous roots, with the name originating from the Aklanon word sangkâ líbo, meaning "one thousand." Legend has it that this name commemorates the purported one thousand native Ati who attended the inaugural Catholic Mass in the area. Over time, Kalibo evolved from its original spelling, Calivo, reflecting the dynamic interplay of linguistic influences and historical narratives that have shaped its identity.

Initially referred to as Akean by its inhabitants, echoing the name of the adjacent river, Kalibo is steeped in linguistic symbolism. The term "akean" evokes the melodic babbling of flowing waters, derived from the Aklanon root word akae, signifying "to boil." This linguistic motif underscores the profound connection between Kalibo's cultural identity and its natural landscape, where the rhythmic ebb and flow of life resonate with the pulse of the river.

Through the ages, Kalibo has been immortalized by a tapestry of names, including Calibo, Daclan, Adan, and Calibog, each bearing testament to the town's enduring legacy and multifaceted history. As Kalibo continues to evolve and embrace the promise of tomorrow, it remains a beacon of resilience, diversity, and cultural heritage in the heart of the Philippines.
